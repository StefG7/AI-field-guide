Foundations

What Is Generative AI,
in Plain Language?

AI is a tool you guide using plain language — like having a conversation. You provide a prompt to explain what you're working on, add context, and give it a task. It's trained on pre-existing knowledge and is good at piecing that information together to best suit your needs. However, it is still a computer, and its thinking is based on mathematical equations that don't always fully understand human intricacies. AI is meant to aid, not replace, your judgment. Always conduct human revision on any AI-generated content.

The Internet Parallel

One clarifying insight from this course: AI feels a lot like the early internet. Incredible potential, fast growth, and a regulatory landscape still catching up.

The Internet (Then)

Amazing promise, real risks

Fast communication, open-source knowledge, search engines. But also data vulnerabilities, spam, fraud — and psychological effects we're still uncovering. Regulation came slowly and the internet is now essential infrastructure.

Generative AI (Now)

Moving even faster

AI has grown so exponentially fast that even regulators struggle to keep up. The same pattern applies: enormous good is possible, but guardrails matter. The goal right now is to use AI responsibly — and to figure out what that really means.

Tool Exploration

AI Tools I've Tried & Tested

I put four tools through their paces with real work tasks. Here's an honest look at the process — what worked, what didn't, and whether I'd use them again.

HeyGen
Content Generation
Good for brief clips. Not ready for full productions yet.
The Prompt

Selected a video style and avatar, then provided an idea about a future where multimedia learning is booming.

The Experience

Normally I write scripts with ChatGPT and source assets myself. HeyGen handled the whole plan in minutes — but avatar eye tracking was strange and the voice had a lisp. A good starting point, but revisions are needed.

Would I Use It?

Yes — for short clips embedded in larger productions. Better at low-motion videos for now. Full-video generation still needs more polish before it's production-ready.

Napkin
Knowledge Discovery · Content Generation
Surprisingly useful for training visuals and cheat sheets.
The Prompt

Pasted step-by-step staff instructions for database inputs — no extra context needed. Napkin's purpose-built design means you don't have to over-explain.

The Experience

Napkin generated multiple flowchart options from the text and I browsed them to find a colorful, easy-to-understand visual. The minimalistic UI was a pleasure to use.

Would I Use It?

Not an everyday tool, but genuinely useful when I host trainings on design platforms like Canva or Adobe Express. These visuals would serve as far better cheat sheets than plain-text instructions.

Gamma
Content Generation · Perception
Great for PPT redesigns — needs more accessibility features.
The Prompt

Uploaded an existing training PPT file. Rather than starting from scratch, I wanted Gamma to help redesign and reformat the slides while keeping the content intact.

The Experience

Gamma accurately understood the slide elements and offered the choice to enhance content or keep it as-is — a critical feature since I'm not allowed to alter curriculum wording.

Would I Use It?

Yes, for PPT redesign requests that come in from trainers and curriculum developers. It can save a lot of time matching branding guidelines — though I'd love to see stronger accessibility tools built in.

Notebook LM
Knowledge Discovery · Content Generation
A new fixture in my scenario-building workflow.
The Prompt

Fed it materials from a hoarding disorder training I attended, then asked it to create a sample scenario between a social worker and a client with hoarding disorder.

The Experience

NotebookLM produced a compelling storyboard with well-developed characters that genuinely evoke empathy. It then summarized that story into an emotion-evoking video meant to draw learners into the narrative.

Would I Use It?

Absolutely — this is now part of my workflow for scenario development. For social worker training especially, empathy-building scenarios are crucial, and NotebookLM handles this surprisingly well.

Critical Thinking

Three Ethics Scenarios
Worth Discussing

In a group discussion with colleagues, we worked through three real-world AI ethics scenarios. These are the questions they raised — and why they matter.

🎙️

Voice Cloning for a University President

A video producer used AI to recreate a university president's voice for a voiceover. The president later approved it — but did they consent before it was made? Our group felt consent must come first, every time. Voice data also carries risk if accessed by the wrong hands, making secure platforms non-negotiable.

📊

AI-Drafted Budget Proposal

Someone used AI to help draft a budget proposal under deadline pressure. Reasonable — but our group felt transparency matters even when a manager reviews the final product. Budget documents also contain sensitive financial data, making tool security and data anonymization critical considerations.

🎓

Student Data & AI Analysis

A staff member used an unapproved tool (found via YouTube) to analyze student demographic and GPA data. Our group raised flags: removing IDs may not fully anonymize the data, and unapproved tools carry unknown data storage risks. Institutional approval matters — especially with student information.

The through-line across all three: The technology itself isn't the problem. It comes down to consent, transparency, and security practices. Used thoughtfully and with the right guardrails, these tools can genuinely help. Without them, they create real risks — for individuals, institutions, and the people they serve.

Organizational Context

AI Policy at My
Department

Here's an honest look at where my organization currently stands on AI practices — what's working, what's missing, and what I'd recommend.

✓ A strength

Data Confidentiality

Fresno State offers ChatGPT EDU accounts that prevent chats from being used to train data. IT also provides trainings on new AI tools. This is personally one of my highest priorities — I work with proprietary curriculum that shouldn't be used to train external models.

⚠ A gap

AI Tool Procurement

My company's affiliation with Fresno State doesn't include access to campus-protected accounts like ChatGPT EDU or Copilot. Most of my team uses personal accounts — which don't come with the same enterprise-level data protections. This is a real vulnerability.

→ My recommendation

Safety Training for Personal Accounts

If enterprise accounts aren't feasible, the next best step is training staff on how to use AI safely with personal accounts. I know the tips — but many colleagues don't. Awareness is the first guardrail we can actually provide right now.

Key Takeaways

Before You
Prompt Away

A few reminders worth carrying into any AI-assisted workflow — especially in higher education and social services contexts.

01

Human Revision is Non-Negotiable

AI doesn't fully understand human intricacies. Every output needs a real human review — especially when accuracy, empathy, or curriculum fidelity are at stake.

02

Consent Before You Clone

Before using someone's voice, likeness, or data — get their consent first. Even if they'd likely approve, the standard should be asking beforehand.

03

Know What You're Feeding It

Sensitive data — student records, budget figures, proprietary curriculum — needs extra care. Check the platform's privacy policy before you paste anything in.

04

Be Transparent About AI Use

Whether it's a proposal, a video, or a discussion post — if AI helped create it, say so. Transparency builds trust and sets a healthy professional norm.

05

Use Approved Tools When Possible

Your campus or IT department may have vetted platforms with built-in data protections. Start there before turning to a free trial you found in a YouTube video.

06

AI Amplifies What You Bring

The best AI outputs come from people who know their subject deeply. Your expertise, judgment, and professional context are what make the results actually good.
